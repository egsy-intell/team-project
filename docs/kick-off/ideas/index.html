Top options

Two viable project directions

Both options can support a practical prediction assignment, but they differ sharply on data friction and how much bespoke assembly the team must do before modeling.

Ranked #1 for decision speed

PFAS occurrence risk in water systems

1

Predict whether a well, sampling point, or water system exceeds a PFAS detection threshold. This is the cleanest choice if your team wants strong policy relevance plus public geospatial data that can be turned into a simple classifier quickly.

Core hypothesis

  • Closer proximity to PFAS-relevant facilities raises detection risk.
  • Industrial or mixed land use around the site increases odds of occurrence.

Main predictors

  • Distance to airports, military sites, or industrial sources
  • Land-use mix in a local buffer
  • Hydrogeologic proxy such as aquifer or soil class

Why it works fast

  • Binary target is easy to define
  • Geospatial features are interpretable
  • Federal and EPA-linked sources reduce label hunting
Ranked #2 for data assembly burden

Crop fungal-disease outbreak risk

2

Predict weekly disease risk for a crop-pathogen pair using weather and pathogen-pressure proxies. The scientific signal is strong, but the biggest question is whether your team can secure a clean labeled outbreak dataset fast enough.

Core hypothesis

  • Outbreaks become more likely when temperature and moisture align with pathogen-optimal conditions.
  • Recent local detections improve prediction beyond weather alone.

Main predictors

  • Temperature risk index or degree-days
  • Humidity or rainfall proxy
  • Recent incidence or spore-pressure proxy

Why it is slightly slower

  • Weather data are easy, labels are harder
  • Need a defined crop and pathogen pair early
  • Best fit if the team is comfortable assembling research data
Decision matrix

Score the options against the assignment constraints

The strongest differentiators are public-data quality and whether the project can move from scoping to a first model in days rather than weeks.

Criterion PFAS prediction Crop disease prediction
Policy relevance / impact Very high. Public-health framing, contamination surveillance, and remediation prioritization are easy to justify. High. Strong climate-adaptation and food-security relevance, especially if framed around prevention.
Signal strength with 2–3 predictors High. Distance to sources and land use have a clear causal story and often produce interpretable models. High. Temperature and moisture are biologically central, especially for fungal disease windows.
Availability of public data Strongest. Federal catalogs, EPA tools, and USGS-linked resources make it easier to build a public-data-first project. Mixed. Weather is abundant, but outbreak labels may require more custom assembly or a benchmark dataset.
Data quality / cleaning burden Moderate and manageable. The work is mainly geospatial joins, buffering, and thresholding labels. Moderate to high. Label definitions, spatial aggregation, and matching weather to outbreak units can slow the team down.

Interpretability for presentation Very strong. Distance-to-source maps and feature importance translate well for a mixed audience. Strong. Weather windows are intuitive, though the pathogen biology may need more explanation.

Recommendation

Start with PFAS unless your team already has a crop dataset

PFAS is the safer choice for a short assignment because the data path is clearer and the problem can be scoped tightly without losing significance. Crop disease becomes equally compelling only if the team can secure labeled outbreak data almost immediately.

Best default pick: PFAS detection-risk classification

Use a state or regional dataset, define a binary label, and build a model around distance to likely sources, local land use, and one hydrogeologic feature. That gives you a crisp hypothesis, clean visuals, and a good chance of having a complete story within two to three weeks.

Team plan

A two-week execution path

If the goal is decision-making, your team should optimize for rapid dataset assembly and an interpretable first model before experimenting with complexity.

1

Lock the target fast

Decide on one geography, one binary label, and no more than three primary predictors. Avoid continuous prediction unless the labels are already clean.

2

Build the minimum viable dataset

Assemble labels and features in a single table first. For PFAS, that likely means wells or systems plus source-distance, land-use, and hydrogeology fields.

3

Prioritize interpretability

Start with logistic regression or tree-based classification and make sure the output explains the hypothesis clearly. A slightly simpler model is better if it shortens cleaning and improves presentation quality.
